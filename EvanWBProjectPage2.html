Some Longer Reviews!

This page collects a hodge-podge of "magazine-style" film, book, food, and music reviews that I composed during my brief period as a stumbling and terribly self-conscious novice writer. They are arranged in no particular order. I hope to begin contributing new pieces to this page in the near future.

Tender is the Night by F. Scott Fitzgerald (Novel, 1934), originally published in December 2018.

Tender is the Night is F. Scott Fitzgerald’s fourth novel and without question the author’s most intensely personal piece of writing. Dick and Nicole Diver, the American expatriate couple living on the French Riviera during the interwar years, are closely modeled after Gerald and Sarah Murphy, intimate friends and frequent hosts of Scott and Zelda Fitzgerald during their dalliances in France in the 1920s. The characters follow the arc of Fitzgerald’s own life during the nine years it took him to complete the book. When Dick turns to alcohol to cope with his deteriorating career and marriage, the protagonist unmistakably begins to bear the traits of his creator.

The stylistic brilliance of the book is largely undisputed. Tender runs through with classic Fitzgeraldian prose, the sort of writing that flows intrepidly while enticing with the possibility of multiple layers of meaning. In an early passage, Fitzgerald transforms a slow moving train into a symbol juxtaposing one country’s insatiable appetite for money and “progress” to another’s peaceful, almost unassuming, contentment:

"Unlike American trains that were absorbed in an intense destiny of their own, and scornful of people on another world less swift and breathless, this train was part of the country through which it passed. Its breath stirred the dust from the palm leaves, the cinders mingled with dry dung in the gardens."

Fitzgerald’s initial description of Nicole Driver is equally memorable, both for the stunning imagery it evokes and for its subtle intimation that Nicole is a product of European artisanship, not American decadence:

"...her face could have been described in terms of conventional prettiness, but the effect was that it had been made first on the heroic scale with strong structure and marking, as if the features and vividness of brow and coloring, everything we associate with temperament and character, had been molded with Rodinesque intention, and then chiseled away in the direction of prettiness to a point where a single slip would have irreparably diminished its force and quality."

Even where Fitzgerald resorts to uncharacteristically cheap methods - for example, that lengthy plot-setting diatribe by Nicole in Book 2 (viii) and the painfully melodramatic parallel he draws between Dick’s faltering love life and a group of Veterans marching for a “lost magnificence, a past effort” - his descent to the realm of the pedestrian remains the exception that proves his superb technique. Maybe it’s enough to say that to write under the name F. Scott Fitzgerald in a post-Gatsby world is to face unreasonable expectations of economy and grace.

But the legacy of this beautifully-written novel is scarred by a matter of form. Fitzgerald’s choice, or maybe his compulsion, to infuse the pages with autobiography has met with vigorous criticism. In a now-famous letter, Ernest Hemingway excoriates Fitzgerald for cheating with his own personal hurt, for starting with Gerald and Sarah Murphy and turning them into something altogether more tragic: Scott and Zelda themselves. Fiedler goes further, stopping short of suggesting that Fitzgerald lived the way he did - the booze, the infidelity, the constant self-deprecation - to authenticate the downfall of Dick Driver.

Both critics are justified. Just about every character in Tender is a stand-in for Fitzgerald. Each is painted with a piece of the man. McKisco gets his inferiority complex and his social awkwardness. Abe North, the alcoholism (and, eerily, the premature death). And Tommy Barban is his masculine foil; he fought the war Fitzgerald avoided (he’s also obviously Ernest Hemingway).

Dick Diver initially represents the personal and professional ideal to which Fitzgerald aspired - a man who dominates with social grace, whose brilliance is kindling and inevitable, and for whom money is not an end but rather a means to cultural progress. At first, Dick is seen through the eyes of Rosemary Hoyt, the virginal young actress whose recent semi-stardom permits her access to Dick and Nicole but of course not inclusion in their mystical covenant. Fitzgerald, who looked in on Hemingway and up at James Joyce, lives in Rosemary too.

This creates an issue of consistency in characterization and ultimately of believability. Albert and Violet McKisco, Mary and Abe North, and Tommy Barban are caricatures of their Fitzgeraldian traits. So when, for example, the bumbling McKisco becomes a best-selling novelist, knowing little about him beyond his dinner table antics, his ascent seems almost satirical. Similarly, the once heroic and imperturbable Dick Diver is unrecognizable as the drunkard and the professional failure he becomes in books II and III. This version of Dick can only be properly understood as F. Scott Fitzgerald himself.

I’ve no doubt that Fitzgerald anticipated the criticism resulting from his deliberate metafictions. I also suspect that he couldn’t help but to write this very story precisely the way that he did. Though it’s often misread as a tale of romantic love, Tender is the Night is really about America. All of Fitzgerald’s writing is about America. And in the America Fitzgerald writes about – where success is illusive, where love is transient, and where most of us remain on the outside looking in, and beating on – the author himself is the quintessential tragic character.

Notes on The Nickel Boys (Novel, 2019), originally published in September 2020

The subject of Colson Whitehead’s thought-provoking new work of historical fiction is a 1960’s era Florida reformatory for truant and criminally involved teenagers. To be sure, The Nickel Academy for Boys — or just “Nickel” to those unlucky enough to have attended the long-standing Florida institution — is a school in name only. Classes are brief reprieves from the main occupation of its students, which is to generate money for the white administrators and trustees. The residents of the entirely segregated Black campus spend most of their days farming cash crops or operating a for-profit printing press (that is, for the exclusive profit of Nickel’s founders and operators). The Black children deemed “trustworthy” enough, such as the main character Elwood, are permitted to travel off campus with a chaperone to sell loads of Nickel’s government-subsidized food and other supplies to local businesses. They eat what’s left over, sometimes just watery gruel. This is the Deep South under Jim Crow, so even the state inspectors nod complicitly at this arrangement.

Nickel is a vestige of Southern Chattel Slavery. It’s founder was a member of the Ku Klux Klan, and the current faculty, if not affiliated with the KKK themselves, are blissfully ignorant to the origin and true purpose of the school. When Elwood and his friend Turner are assigned to clean out the basement of a former county employee and Nickel “booster,” they find a stack of old Imperial Nighthawks newsletters, one of the official publications of the Klan. It’s no surprise that the benefactor’s father was in his own time an “avid proponent of peonage,” often employing paroled students from Nickel to tend his farms and chickens before this form of indentured servitude was officially banned. The school’s existence — more pointedly, the filial bonds that perpetuate it and the financial and political corruption that sustain it — demonstrates how seamlessly racial exploitation has continued even after the abolition of slavery.

Some of the references to plantation life are obvious: the uncompensated physical labor, the prohibition of educational materials, corporal punishment (to put it lightly). Others are more subtle. For example, the school employs a system of social promotion that rewards “demonstrations of compliance or docility.” To graduate before the age of 18, the mandatory age of matriculation at Nickel, Elwood must be promoted to “Ace,” a distinction awarded to those who have shown acquiescence to their oppressors. At first, this feature of Nickel’s “curriculum” somewhat comports with Elwood’s affinity for the teachings of Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr. He frequently reminds himself of Dr. King’s admonition to love one’s enemy:

“Throw us in jail and we will still love you. Bomb our homes and threaten our children, and, as difficult as it is, we will still love you…drag us out onto some wayside road, and beat us and leave us half-dead, and we will still love you.”

But when the violence of his wardens becomes too much to bear, Elwood all but abandons this doctrine of nonviolent resistance. As on the plantation, violence, at least the threat of it, is the primary method of coercion at Nickel. “The White House” is a small, inconspicuous barn on the Black side of the compound where the literal whippings take place. Here, recalcitrant students are forced to lay on a filthy, blood-stained mattress while they are lashed unconscious. This is at any rate preferable to being taken “out back.” That’s where incorrigibles — such as the young Black student who fails to take a dive in a boxing match against a white boy — are bound by their limbs to a set of trees and beaten to death. Their “disappearance” is always either unreported or they’re labeled as runaways.

Whitehead is aware of the centrality of violence to the maintenance of the vast system of racial oppression that has existed in the United States since the very first settlement. As the story’s narrator puts it, “violence is the only lever big enough to move the world.” And certainly the propensity or capacity for violence is the only evolutionary advantage that whites can claim over anyone else. At Nickel, the brutal methods of the slaveholders of generations past are inherited and passed down. The methods remain unchanged except for the secrecy with which they’re administered, usually under the guise of night, in a secluded location, the screams of the victims drowned out by a massive industrial fan. On the occasion of Elwood’s final beating, Spencer, the school’s main disciplinarian, eventually bequeaths his whip, cruelly nicknamed “Black Beauty,” and the prowess with which he wields it, to a new hire. The transaction conveys the ease with which violent institutions are kept alive over time. All of this cleverly presages how racial violence in the post-Civil Rights era has been modified to accommodate our present sensibilities. Nowadays, official violence persists, codified in law by terms such as “justifiable force.”

The story alternately glimpses at life after Nickel, life in the “free world” as the students used to refer to it in their fantasies. Symbolically emulating the exodus of emancipated slaves to the urban North East, one escapee relocates to New York City and over the course of a few decades builds a successful small business and even marries. Indeed, he’s an anomaly; many, if not most, of his Nickel peers have died from drug overdose or street violence, if they managed to escape the cycle of incarceration initiated in childhood. Whitehead is careful not to portray the former student’s success as a full recovery from his traumatic days at Nickel. Doing so might legitimize the ridiculous notion of a level post-Civil Rights playing field — that is, the idea that the end of segregation remedied the economic and emotional devastation wrought by legal discrimination in America. He’s initially relegated to backbreaking minimum-wage labor and tenement-style living like so many victims of generational poverty, but he suffers in other ways that are unseen, ways that are difficult to quantify in arguments about the legacy of slavery. His equanimity betrays a deep-seated sense of exclusion. In the midst of an impromptu meeting with another Nickel alumnus named “Chickie,” an unemployed transient who suppresses his own trauma beneath a peculiar but familiar gregariousness, the man has a vision:

“...of himself outlined in bright red, a shimmering aura that marked him as an outsider. Everybody saw it, just like he knew Chickie’s story in two notes. They’d always be on the lamb, no matter how they got out of that school.”

The Nickel Academy actually existed. It was called the Dozier School for Boys, located in Marianna, Florida. In 2012 a group of archaeology students from the University of South Florida discovered the unmarked graves of the children who had been taken “out back” many years ago by the Spencers and Earls and Hennepins of Dozier. An investigation into their deaths ensued, but no concrete links to faculty members could be established. The story of The Nickel Academy might be easier to stomach if it were a time piece, if its existence represented a bygone era clearly distinguishable from our own. But Nickel bears too many likenesses to modern institutions, in particular our ever-expanding prison industrial complex. Draconian drug-sentencing laws aimed at petty inner-city offenders have replaced the capricious and deliberately race-based justice of Elwood’s time as a significant source of the prison system’s low wage labor force. The new method of recruitment is no less effective. According to a recent NYT op-ed, the War on Drugs has ensured that there are now more “ex-cons” in the US than high school graduates, a disproportionate number of them from minority communities. The incentive remains the same: the enrichment of wealthy elites. The influence of slavery’s legacy families has receded, but it has been supplanted by corporations which now own significant stakes in the operations of local, state and Federal jails throughout the country.

I confess to feeling unusually self-aware while reading about Nickel. I’ve spent most of my twelve year career working with pre-release inmates and criminal defendants in New Jersey, first as a counselor in a state-funded drug rehab and later as the director of a large urban Drug Court. I shudder at certain coincidences. “Reckless eyeballing” calls for a whipping at Nickel. As recently as 2005 the very same behavior was warned against in the program manuals of some Therapeutic Community-style treatment facilities in my home state. “Work Therapy,” a popular model of care for addicts involved in the criminal justice system, combines substance abuse counseling with unpaid vocational “training” or “internships,” activities that might easily be justified as “introducing the students to a useful trade in which to apply themselves after graduation.” The phrase in quotations is not my own; its taken directly from a copy of the Nickel brochure that Elwood finds in the infirmary. There’s even a minimum-security prison in South Jersey commonly referred to as “Jones Farm,” where inmates are compensated for their good behavior with on-the-job agricultural training. I’m suspicious of how well this arrangement serves those returning to their homes in the densely populated urban centers of North Jersey. At any rate, it benefits someone.

After the shallow graves of generations of Nickel’s victims are unearthed, a number of former students are invited back to the area to testify to the brutality of the institution. One survivor lodges in The Richmond Hotel, its name perhaps derived from the state in which the first American settlement, and the first trade in human slaves, occurred. The narrator describes the recently remodeled hotel as a “Tallahassee landmark.”

He adds that “great care had gone into preserving the grand old establishment.”

Notes on Joker (Film, 2019), originally published in February 2020

Most critical responses to Joker (2019) include some reference to Taxi Driver (1976), Martin Scorsese’s classic story of another deranged loner with a savior complex. Oddly, these reviews mostly fail to mention an equally significant analogue: Charlie Chaplin’s Modern Times (1936). The fundraiser at which Arthur Flek/Joker confronts his believed-to-be estranged father Thomas Wayne is in fact a viewing of that very film. In Modern Times, the Tramp, the indefatigable protagonist of nearly all of Chaplin’s movies, ends up bouncing between jail and the streets after losing his job as an assembly line worker in a state-of-the-art production facility. The hilarious opening sequence shows the Tramp struggling to keep up with an ever-quickening conveyor belt. Moments later he’s subjected to a crude experiment involving a feeding machine designed to eliminate lunch breaks. The tragic subtext of these anecdotes is the immense suffering wrought to middle and lower class people by the American manufacturing colossus at the height of the industrial era.

Replace the unfeeling factory system of the 1930’s with the callous public service bureaucracies of the late Twentieth Century and you have the backdrop to Arthur Flek's own demise. The brilliant irony of Joker is that its anti-hero, played masterfully by Joaquin Phoenix, is in great part compelled to violence by the self-important do-gooders who purport to uplift the downtrodden. “You don’t listen, do you? I don’t think you ever really listened to me,” he admonishes his unmistakably disinterested social worker. Though she herself is the victim of a city government that recently cut funding for social services, costing her a job and Arthur his much-needed medication, the woman’s indifference is inexcusable. (A professionally-trained social worker myself, I squirmed at all of the missed opportunities to preempt Arthur’s rapid decline into violence.) Though filmed over 80 years ago in the extremely nascent era of mental health awareness, Modern Times addresses the same issue. In an effort to maintain the breakneck pace of the assembly line, the Tramp works himself into a literal stupor. His symptoms are exaggerated and intended to be symbolic of the anxiety-inducing conditions of factory labor; but, still, they’re largely ignored. While Modern Times concentrates our anger on a society that couldn’t care less about the mental health of its citizens, Joker, in much more nuanced fashion, draws our attention to one that pretends to care about mental health, but actually doesn’t. There’s something uniquely alarming about the latter.

Even the benevolent labor unions are seen as complicit in Arthur’s downfall. The garbage strike alluded to at the beginning of Joker – a clear reference to the New York City trash strike of 1968 – is mostly interpreted as evidence of the fomenting discontent that eventually unites Joker’s adherents. But while the union bosses, those so-called champions of the working class, hold out for better wages, the residents of the city, especially those employed on the fringes of society like Arthur, are left to deal with the physical and emotional consequences of living beneath heaps of waste. A city that can little afford to manage its own trash can hardly be expected to address the complicated needs of a person like Arthur. Not to mention, one may reasonably infer a connection between the union’s demands and the city government’s decision to divert funds away from Arthur’s mental health treatment program. Even Charlie Chaplin, a strident supporter of the political left, seems to have understood the smallness of man in an era of mass movements. This theme pervades Modern Times. In one of the film’s legendary moments, the Tramp inadvertently joins a communist protest and is erroneously pegged as its leader, leading to his incarceration. In another scene he joins a workers strike and is again falsely arrested. In both instances, the Tramp is swept up by the fervor of the movement only to suffer its consequences all by himself.

I avoided seeing Joker on opening weekend for my disdain of crowded theaters and the very audible chewing noises that make public movie-watching extremely difficult for me. But I remained carefully attuned to the reactions of my peers in the days and weeks following the film’s release. Most of them saw it as a film about poor people rising up against rich people exemplified by the way that Joker’s followers turned against the “finance bros” of Gotham City. After seeing it for myself, I took every opportunity to point out that Arthur doesn’t hate Thomas Wayne because he’s rich; he hates the man because, well, he won’t give him a hug. If there is a single factor that’s determinate in the divergent paths taken by Joker and the Tramp it’s that the unfailingly resolute and irrepressibly cheerful hero of Modern Times finds the one thing thing that Arthur doesn’t: the genuine and unqualified affection of another human being. And what makes the absence of affection so tragic in Arthur’s case is that he was raised in a world that purports to cherish it.

Notes on Never, Rarely, Sometimes, Always (Film, 2019), originally published in August 2020

The Jay Roach-directed Bombshell (2020), a campy and occasionally melodramatic retelling of the events leading to Roger Ailes’s ousting from Fox News, is Hollywood’s first attempt to directly address the sexual abuse scandals that rocked the entertainment industry beginning in 2016. Roach, of Austin Powers, Borat, and Bruno fame, has a penchant for audacious humor and political satire and of late is interested in large-scale institutional takedowns (see also Mark Felt: The Man Who Brought Down the White House). Not surprisingly, then, Bombshell is more concerned with spinning a suspenseful tale (and guffawing at the clueless Fox employees on screen) than it is with faithfully depicting female trauma. There are enough feminist tropes and sentimental refrains to register its creators’ ostensible support for #MeToo, but these moments are absorbed into the film’s overriding atmosphere of caricature. The effect of depicting Sean Hannity (who is ridiculous enough in real life) with a Glock protruding from the waistline of his trousers as he rails against the “snowflakes” in his own backyard is to mock the whole affair, enjoining the viewer to say, “In the end, we got our guy, so let’s have a laugh about it and forget this ever happened.”

Even some of the more respectable Hollywood productions of the last few years reflect the industry’s mealy-mouthedness on the struggle for women’s liberation. Greta Gerwig’s adaptation of Little Women (2019) is a spectacular period piece with weighty performances to match its stunning cinematography. Despite these achievements, the film emphasizes everything that is polite about Louisa May Alcott’s 1868 novel while minimizing many of its proto-feminist elements. As one critic astutely observes:

"Gerwig’s Jo is played by Saoirse Ronan without Jo’s boyish mannerisms or speech, or even her clumsiness. Instead we get fourth-wave-feminist Jo…beautiful, strong-willed, wanting to be a writer, ready to take on a world that is not ready for her. But this is nonsense; Jo’s vocation is hardly an act of rebellion; writing fiction was one of the few occupations considered suitable for a gentlewoman in the 19th century. What drew generations of readers to Alcott’s Jo is that she is a girl who wants to be a boy."

It appears the Yeoman’s work will be left to the outsiders, those on the ground level of the feminist movement in the visual arts. One such noble laborer is Australian-born filmmaker and screenwriter Kitty Green, whose latest offering, The Assistant, is a major film with a deliberately minor plot. In it, Julia Garner plays a recently hired personal assistant at a New York-based film production company. The film follows Garner’s character over the course of one long workday as she performs demeaning janitorial work and is subjected to serial micro aggressions by her mostly young male coworkers. She is aware that her boss (a lightly veiled Harvey Weinstein) preys on young actresses, trading opportunities for sex, but she remains casually complicit in, if deeply conflicted by, this grotesque subterfuge. Absent a traditional storyline, the viewer is drawn into the casual gestures and transactions that ultimately sustain institutional abuse and oppression. In this way, every frame carries weight; a crumpled-up paper bag thrown at the main character by her blithe male officemate lands like an anvil on an unsuspecting passerby. The day-in-the-life-of approach reminds us that sexual abuse isn’t limited to headline-worthy events – for many women, it’s an everyday affair.

The Assistant is an important film, but it is in some ways overly constructed. Green deliberately obscures the identity of the villain, ensuring through deft camera cuts that we don’t ever see the CEO’s face. We even struggle to hear what he says to his assistant on the telephone, his voice muffled and made nearly inaudible by design. The viewer must piece together his personality and the details of his deranged sexual behavior through clues in his environment, including the reactions of his subordinates. Green’s intent here is to represent the “office” as a sort of composite character. The point is that bad men are abetted by systems, which are ultimately made of people who keep quiet for any number of reasons. As an idea, this is interesting. But our constant awareness of the director’s calculations and “touch” ultimately reduces the film’s emotional impact and resonance.

Under different circumstances I might’ve overlooked this detail, but The Assistant was followed up closely by, and to some extent demands comparison to, Brooklyn-based filmmaker Eliza Hittman’s Never Rarely Sometimes Always, a brilliantly understated tale of one young girl’s odyssey from Pennsylvania to New York City to terminate her unwanted pregnancy. Hittman, whose oeuvre consists of only three feature length films, has already earned a reputation as one of the most subversive and evocative new filmmakers in America, her stories unflinchingly depicting the inner lives of epicene teenagers and young adults in the throes of sexual discovery. I am hard-pressed to use the word “style” in any characterization of Hittman’s work because her perspective is inherently anti-formalist; she rejects standard notions of representation – symbolism, allegory, exposition, imitation (all typified by the films mentioned at the beginning of this review) – in favor of a stripped down quasi-documentary approach.

The most salient feature of this approach is Hittman’s awareness of the potential for dialogue to trivialize and obfuscate meaning. For example, in her second offering, Beach Rats (2017), the film’s protagonist is tortured by the conflict between his attraction to men (which he satisfies by secretly wandering a gay chat room at night) and the heteronormative values of his traditional, mostly Eastern European, Brooklyn community. At the film’s emotional climax, the main character’s mother, realizing that her son’s ongoing silence masks a hidden truth, begs him to “spit it out.” He refuses to speak; this prolongs his despair and denies the viewer the sort of neat and modulated resolution that, while typical of Hollywood happy endings, ultimately inhibits real pathos and empathy. Silence functions in a similar way, though I think to even more dramatic effect, in Never Rarely Sometimes Always. Autumn (Sidney Flanigan), seventeen years old and harboring the secret of an unintended pregnancy, talks to others only when she has to – to placate her mother, to arrange a pregnancy test at a local women’s health center, and eventually to plan her trip to a Manhattan abortion clinic where she hopes to be treated without having to obtain parental consent. There are occasional angry outbursts, mostly directed at her male abusers, but even these are brief and subdued, tempered by Autumn’s deliberate reluctance to speak. Her silence doesn’t prove an incapacity for expression and isn’t merely a symptom of trauma. Rather it is a response to the insufficiency of language to explain or resolve her suffering.

So much of what is spoken in the film is banal (a disapproving doctor telling Autumn that her attitude towards motherhood will change once she gives birth), subtly manipulative (a creepy boy trying to impress Autumn’s cousin with the superfluous observation that “New York has a lot of secrets, subterranean levels…”), or disingenuous (Autumn’s mother imploring the girl’s abusive father to “tell her how good she was” in the school talent show). Thus our strong emotional response to the events on screen derives not from words, but almost entirely from images. In one of the film’s most distressing scenes, Autumn repeatedly pounds on her bare stomach in an attempt to kill the fetus inside of her body. Later, in another act of deliberate self-harm, Autumn pierces her own nose. The camera concentrates on the clothespin as it slowly punctures her flesh. Both images cause a reaction in the viewer that no amount of dialogue is capable of provoking: the instinct to turn away. It’s significant to note that in each instance, what we are inclined to turn away from – what we are almost unable to bear – is the physical pain a young girl endures while attempting to assert a modicum of control over her own biological reality.

Two back-to-back viewings of Never Rarely Sometimes Always – and a definite sense that I had seen something truly original – inspired me to re-watch what I suppose is the most popular American film about teen pregnancy, Jason Reitman’s Academy Award-winning Juno (2007). This time around, perhaps owing to Hittman’s impression on me, I immediately recognized the film’s uneasiness around the issue of women’s self-determination, manifested by an excessive, almost unrelenting, “talkativeness.” Juno (Ellen Page) fills up each scene with such preternatural intelligence and wit that she seems readymade for teen pregnancy. I chuckled when the much older soon-to-be adoptive father of her unborn baby gifts Juno a comic book titled Most Fruitful Yuki; it’s a tale about a pregnant superhero. And so is Juno. This is fine except that the film’s deliberate attempt to cloak its protagonist’s trauma in sheer glibness forbids intimacy, or any emotional response other than amusement. It is precisely the type of film to which Hittman’s approach directly responds, one in which dialogue dilutes emotion and creates distance, perhaps intentionally.

Around the same time that I first viewed Never Rarely Sometimes Always, I wrapped up Susan Sontag’s early collection of film and literary criticism, Against Interpretation. In these writings, the famous polemic (and cinephile) reminds us that great art, rather than merely delivering ideas, educates conscience and sensibility. Certainly there are “ideas” and footprints of politics in Never Rarely. Arranged side by side, the adoption-minded health clinic in Autumn’s Pennsylvania home town and the facility where she ends up seeking an abortion in New York reflect the long-standing political institutions undergirding them both. But where Hittman could easily proselytize the virtue of one or the other, she does not. She transcends politics. Ultimately, Never Rarely Sometimes Always achieves something far beyond the realm of the political: it broadens our capacity for empathy by insisting that we focus on the unvarnished details of its protagonist’s life. Every – little – excruciating – detail.

Notes on The Irishman (Film, 2019), originally published in August 2020

The chief complaint about Scorsese’s sweeping new mob epic seems to be that it’s too long. Officially registering at 210 minutes, The Irishman is only a half an hour longer than Casino. Perhaps more tellingly, it's a full 19 minutes shorter than Once Upon a Time in America, Italian filmmaker Sergio Leone's 1984 crime drama also starring Joe Pesci and Robert DeNiro. That film was chopped by 90 minutes to accommodate impatient American audiences, and the result was a commercial and critical failure. Roger Ebert, who lauded the extended version of Once Upon a Time in America, wrote that the original is only too long in the sense that it takes "real concentration to understand [the story's] construction." Indeed, this says more about the taste and attention span of American filmgoers than it does about the quality of the project.

I believe the same logic applies to The Irishman. What many critics are actually saying, whether they realize it or not, is that The Irishman is not the gangster flic they're used to, and it's certainly not the type of mafia movie they’ve come to expect from the director of Mean Streets, Goodfellas, and Casino. Most noticeably, it lacks the glow and freneticism of the aforementioned works. Bright-colored suits, sparkling jewelry, and neon lights - the typical vestments of mob life as seen through Scorsese’s eyes - are replaced by a palette of dull earth tones. Ditto for the film's score. Scorsese remains faithful to the Doo Wop music of the mob's heyday but for this occasion prefers some of the genre’s more somber selections. In the film's opening scene, "In the Still of the Night" whines along lugubriously as the camera slowly searches the hallways of a nursing home for our wheelchair-bound narrator, former mob enforcer Frank Sheeran (DeNiro).

Sheeran was a real mafia hitman employed by Philly mob boss Russell Bufalino, and The Irishman is based on Sheeran's account of events as documented by Charles Brandt in a book titled I Hear You Paint Houses. Purportedly, those are the words legendary Teamster President Jimmy Hoffa uttered to Sheeran before asking him to serve as his personal bodyguard. ("Painting houses" is a euphemism for murder for hire.) The film depicts Sheeran’s life from his fateful first encounter with Russ Bufalino (Joe Pesci) to his release from prison and his elderly convalescence. Along the way, he kills anyone Russ asks him to and develops a close friendship with Hoffa (Al Pacino), whose connection to the mob involves loaning huge sums of money from the Teamster pension fund to wise guys in the casino business.

The film’s funereal atmosphere is partly owed to the fact that we meet our protagonists a bit later on in life and see most of them through to old age. Steely-eyed killers though they are, Russ and Frank complain about cataracts, fall asleep during long car rides (mouth agape like my father ten minutes into a Turner Classic), and make health-conscious decisions like eating Corn Flakes or Total for breakfast. The much talked about de-aging technology, which increased the film’s budget to well over $175 million, has its limitations; even in flashbacks to Russ and Frank’s younger years, both appear strikingly mature for their supposed ages. The film laughs at itself when Russ refers to Frank as “kid” during their chance introduction at a roadside truck stop. This is all by design, of course. The Irishman eschews the youthful exuberance epitomized by characters like Johnny Boy Civello and Henry Hill. The opening monologue to Goodfellas – “As far back as I can remember, I always wanted to be a gangster” – portends a story of hope and idealism. Frank Sheeran’s deep, plodding voice at the beginning of The Irishman - “I was one of a thousand working stiffs, until I wasn’t no more, and then I started painting houses” – inverts Ray Liotta’s iconic line, conveying a sense of the inexorable, a forfeiture to what quickly becomes an unavoidable fate.

This fate involves lots of bloodshed. Violence features prominently in all of Scorsese’s work within the genre. Though not on the order of Quentin Tarantino’s borderline sadism, Scorsese’s brand of violence is loud, colorful, audacious, sometimes even humorous. But here there are no gruesome bludgeonings, or hands in vice grips, or frozen corpses hanging on meat racks. Frank Sheeran is as prolific and efficient a killer as any of Scorsese’s enforcers, but he’s also the most subdued and reluctant. Distress issues from the cracks in his stoic expression anytime Russ orders a hit. A silent plea for the lives of the ill-fated? Frank distracts himself from the terror of his occupation by performing it with mechanical precision, but one still gets the sense that he suffers immensely for every life that he takes. This is a reckoning of sorts for Scorsese, whose strongmen are usually Machiavellian sociopaths incapable of remorse. Tommy Devito (Goodfellas) and Nicky Santoro (Casino) are evil incarnate, and arguably they both meet fates proportionate to their terrible deeds. But their one-dimensional natures and gruesome deaths reduce them to a facile, and by now redundant, morality lesson. They’re fun to watch for a while, but we can’t put ourselves in their shoes because both lack any semblance of humanity. Frank Sheeran represents something closer to home and altogether more terrifying: the possibility that pathological violence and real conscience can coexist.

Gangster films have always been fertile territory for the study of psychopathology and individual deviance. They tend to address variations of the same question: “What makes people behave this way?” The Irishman, however, displays a deeper consciousness of the origins of moral corruption. It transcends the genre by asking, “How can a modern democratic nation behave this way?” Looming mysteriously above the action throughout much of the film are the “old timers,” presumably the elusive heads of the major crime families, and “the old man,” the patriarch of the Kennedy political dynasty. Together they orchestrate deals to subvert American democracy and advance their mutual political and criminal interests. Whereas gangster films usually toy with the notion of such a conspiracy – a duffel bag on the doorstep or a hooker to the hotel room of a Senator – The Irishman treats the issue with a deadpan seriousness. Russ spearheads a voter fraud campaign to elect JFK in hopes that he’ll reciprocate by ousting Fidel Castro (so the mob can return its casinos to Cuba). Frank helps the cause, albeit unwittingly, by delivering a truckload of military-grade weapons to a CIA-sponsored anti-Castro militia. Scorsese even treats us to actual historical footage of a congressional hearing involving the CIA agent alleged to have helped Frank deliver the materiel. (To be certain, the film makes no claim to accuracy, and Sheeran’s accounting of events is regarded by many as dubious. Yet, there can be no doubt that the mob was highly influential in American politics during the Kennedy era.) It’s a subtle trick of Scorsese’s that we never meet “the old timers” or “the old man.” Their mysteriousness imbues them with power and a sense of permanence, and in this way their covenant symbolizes the commingling of crime and politics at the highest level of governance since a time long before our own.

Even where the film appears to be lamely moralizing against its characters, it’s actually up to something more clever. Frank’s daughter Peggy (Lucy Gallina and Ana Paquin) has been called the film’s “moral barometer” or “moral center.” As a young child she is keenly aware of her father’s violent underside. In her taciturn demeanor towards Frank and Russ we see real fear and loathing. On the other hand, she’s enamored of Jimmy Hoffa from the moment she meets the charismatic Teamster President. In the little girl’s eyes, Uncle Jimmy is the antithesis of her own father: a family man, a hard worker, and a defender of the working class. It is on the day of Hoffa’s notorious disappearance that Peggy (by now an adult) stops talking to her father altogether. She discerns in Frank’s restless and evasive manner that he knows where Jimmy Hoffa is, and that he’s probably the one who put him there. Indeed the temptation to empathize with Peggy is strong. But to do so is to partake in the very myth-making about American innocence that Scorsese aims to upend. In her adoration of Hoffa – who evangelizes justice for the working man and then uses Teamster pension money to fund violent criminal enterprises; who relies on mafia hit men to “eliminate” his private sector competitors; and who brazenly tries to persuade Frank to kill a made man who presents a threat to his hold on power – Peggy, the moral anchor of the story, represents a certain irreconcilable conflict of American identity: who the hell are we to judge with all this blood on our hands?

Notes on An American Tragedy (Novel, 1925), originally published in October 2018

Theodore’s Dreiser’s An American Tragedy was published in 1925, a crowded year for novels about the moral confusion of the early Twentieth Century. That same year Dos Passos achieved his first commercial success with Manhattan Transfer, Hemingway broke ground on his debut novel, The Sun Also Rises (though it wouldn’t be published until 1926), and F. Scott Fitzgerald, for his part, slipped in a little story called The Great Gatsby. It might be fair to say that An American Tragedy, though ambitious in length at 866 pages (the Signet Classic version), is the least talked-about of the aforementioned works. Its legacy has mostly been sustained by the second of two film adaptations, A Place in the Sun (1951), a cinematic treasure starring Liz Taylor and Monty Clift.

The reason for the book’s relative obscurity is pretty simple: it’s badly written. That’s not just my opinion either. In fact, it’s a sort of consensus among critics. Even Dreiser’s close friend, the literary critic H.L. Mencken, couldn’t conceal his frustration, calling An American Tragedy a “heaping cartload of raw materials for a novel, with rubbish of all sorts intermixed.” It certainly doesn’t take a huge literary mind like Mencken’s to notice that An American Tragedy, especially the first 300 pages, is ostentatious, redundant, muddled, just plain overwritten. Passages like this one aren’t confusing, they’re dizzying:

“For following the evening at the Shuman home, and because, in spite of Clyde’s hesitation at the time, all three including Rita herself, were still convinced that he must or would be smitten with her charms, there had been various hints, as well as finally a direct invitation or proposition on the part of Dillard to the effect that because of the camaraderie which had been established between himself and Clyde and these two girls, they make a weekend trip somewhere — preferably to Utica or Albany.”

And this sort of writing abounds.

Dreiser’s style throughout much of the book plays like a cheap Victorianism. The problem with this, aside from the fact that he sounds like a poor man’s Dickens, is that the story of Clyde Griffiths, the attractive youth of humble origins who’s seduced by the prospect of wealth and kills his pregnant girlfriend to make room for the rich and beautiful Sondra Finchley, is a thoroughly modern one. Down to Dreiser’s word choice, this conflict between the author’s edgy subject matter and his old-fashioned parlance is evident. In one passage the author describes the atmosphere as “caloric” (he means hot and stuffy), a term derived from a theory of heat production that was disproven by the beginning of the 19th century. A voluptuous woman is said, with unbelievable prudishness, to have a “fleshy solidity and foreign flavor” and her friend to possess “French shoulders.” And maybe worst of all, Dreiser uses “tergiversation” more than once to characterize Clyde’s habit of evading his forsaken lover. Why can’t Clyde evade or equivocate? Why must he tergiversate? For that matter, when was the last time any character in American literature tergiversated?

If you don’t see it yourself just yet, hold Dreiser’s stuffiness up against Fitzgerald in The Great Gatsby. In a lot of instances they’re treading on very similar themes. Their characters are defined by selfishness and a general lack of regard for the consequences of their behavior. Dreiser, making this point about Clyde, writes boringly: “His was a disposition which did not tend to load itself with more than the most immediate cares.”

Now take that famous line of Fitzgerald’s about Daisy and Tom:

“They were careless people, Tom and Daisy — they smashed up things and creatures and retreated back into their money or their vast carelessness or whatever it was that kept them together, and let other people clean up the mess they made”

Fitzgerald’s prose reflects the coolness, the fluidity, the obliviousness of his characters and, more broadly, the social circles in which they travel. It’s easy to see why Gatsby got a modern silver screen remake featuring Leo DiCaprio. No, I don’t think Gatsby is timeless, as its diminishing returns with young people today seem to show, but it is readable, digestible, relatable today. The same can’t be said so confidently about An American Tragedy.

But this doesn’t mean that we shouldn’t read it. The constant tension between old and new in Dreiser’s voice, frustrating as it may be for his readers, is actually inseparable from his theme. Whereas Gatsby picks up with characters who are blithely rewriting the social contracts of American culture, rejecting Prohibition and the rural Puritanism that inspired it, Dreiser tells the story of a country not fully given over, one still caught in between the forces of traditionalism and modernity. This story is told through the personal and institutional conflicts that drive the plot: the conflict between Clyde and his devoutly Christian family, between poor farmers and rich city dwellers, between moral justice and legal justice, and between a Governor who would spare Clyde’s life and a religious confidante who can persuade him to do so, but whose faith won’t allow it.

You can even sense a progression in Dreiser’s style when these conflicts come into greater focus, and especially when the author himself clashes with tradition. His description of death row is harrowing and betrays his true feelings about the age-old practice of electrocuting criminals. He discards the gaudiness of the early chapters in favor of something plainer and more literal — more modern, you might even say:

“So this was what death was like here; men cried, prayed, they lost their minds — yet the deadly process was in no way halted, for all their terror. Instead at ten o’clock and in order to quiet all those who were left, a cold lunch was brought in and offered — but with none eating save the Chinaman over the way.”

Notice the men weren’t “dispossessed of their mental faculties” or “relieved of their sanity.” Rather, the author who gives us “tergiversate” a half dozen times in the same book prefers in this instance that they simply “lost their minds.”

Dreiser was wrapping things up when the so-called Modernists, among them James Joyce, Virginia Woolf, and TS. Elliot, burst onto the scene in the 1920’s. An American Tragedy would be his last major work, and in the years following its publication he would make no effort to integrate the experimental approaches to consciousness, form, and style exemplified by his successors. So, it’s no wonder that Dreiser is portrayed today as a traditional figure, a writer who was distinctly and obstinately outside of the avant-garde. But this opinion ignores the reality that literary revolutions are progressive. That they result, often slowly, from unresolved tension and conflict. An American Tragedy, for all of its flaws, is teeming with the sort of tensions that the Modernists would eventually stretch to their breaking points. It might even be said, to borrow Mencken’s phrase about the novel, that Dreiser’s last great work supplies the “raw materials” for the revolution in literary fiction that surrounded it.

Notes on Kafka on the Shore (Novel, 2002), originally published in December 2018

I discovered Haruki Murakami in a bookstore near the Manilla Bay region of the Philippines. Like an oddness you might experience in a Murakami novel, the store was plopped between a used tire shop and a dimly lit day spa. Beyond the glass door entrance were a few mismatched wooden tables containing a hodgepodge of new and used books, shrapnel of genres, subject matter, and authors really, and behind this a few shabbily constructed Formica shelves holding cheap stationary and even cheaper tourist ephemera. This all seemed to be a pretense for the store’s main feature, a 6 or 7-foot Tipi-shape bookshelf featuring new Vintage Classics editions of all of Murakami’s novels. The author on display felt important, but I couldn’t reconcile the absurdity of the covers and plot descriptions with the kind of popularity that justifies such a fantastic display.

With few other options for reading materials awaiting me aboard the USS Bonhomme Richard, I eeny-meeny-miny-moed my selection and ended up with a copy of The Wind-Up Bird Chronicle. I started the book when I returned to the ship that day and read it over the next two months, finishing it right before the end of my unit’s deployment to the region. When I got some free Wi-Fi during a stopover in Pattaya Beach, Thailand, I wrote a generally unfavorable review of the book on Goodreads, conceding that my lack of experience with Japanese fiction probably prevented me from appreciating one of Murakami’s most celebrated novels. This is what I said:

“I arrived to the book’s ending feeling that too much of the plot had been untied, too much of the author’s foreshadowing left unfulfilled, too many of his symbols and metaphors left unexplained, and too much of it all left to my own broad interpretations.”

I was intent on abandoning Murakami altogether until a respected Goodreads interlocutor intervened in the comments under my review of Wind Up Bird. He assured me that Kafka on the Shore is the author’s masterpiece and the “key to all of his other writing.” So, a few weeks later, sensing that maybe I’d missed something in my first Murakami reading, I picked up a paperback of Kafka from the Marine Corps Base Hawaii library and read it compulsively during periods of severe sleeplessness, one of the more unfortunate consequences of post-deployment jet lag.

I had a similar, though not quite as depressing, experience with Kafka, published almost a decade after Wind Up Bird. To explain the novel’s bizarre plot as succinctly as possible, Kafka is the story of a young boy, the eponymous Kafka Tamura, who runs away from home to fulfill (and at the same time avoid!) a bizarre prophecy ordained by his abusive father. It’s prophesized that the boy will kill his father and sleep with his own mother and sister. Contemporaneously, an old man named Nakata, who is “stupid” but can speak to cats after a childhood accident involving a UFO, kill’s the young boy’s father himself – well, his spirit kills him in a dream, though the death manifests in reality – and embarks upon a journey to obtain an object (he won’t know that the object is until he sees it) in order to open up, and then quickly close, another “realm” for a purpose that is still unclear at the end of the novel. A sexually ambiguous librarian and an attractive but mysterious library owner who may or may not be the young boy’s long-departed mother also factor prominently into the story.

I was comforted to discover that I am not the only reader who was initially distracted by Murakami’s signature abstractions in Kafka on the Shore. David Mitchell of The Guardian, reviewing the book in 2005, writes:

“Unless I am being particularly dimwitted, loose ends remain far looser than in any Murakami novel to date. The wartime X-File is revisited only once, the UFO is never explained, and the spectral village between the worlds serves little discernible function, beyond being a place for Kafka to escape to and then a place to escape from.”

He goes on:

“For Murakami’s devotees, this fantasy’s loose ends will tantalise; to his admirers, they may invite flummoxed interpretation; but for the unconvinced, they will just dangle, rather ropily.”

While I don’t agree that the loose ends in Kafka are far looser than those in any other novels – in fact, I think Kafka is much tidier than Wind Up Bird in this regard – I found myself bending the limits of my imagination to perceive the meaning of certain objects and characters (e.g. “Colonel Sanders,” the flute made of cats’ souls, the slimy organism that crawls out of Nakata’s mouth after he dies, etc.). John Updike, in his review of Kafka in The New Yorker, attempts to explain this tendency of American readers like me to sigh in confusion at Murakami’s style:

“The novel quotes Goethe as decreeing, ‘Everything’s a metaphor.” But a Western reader expects the metaphors, or symbolic realities...to be organized by certain polarities, in a magnetic field shaped by a Central Supernatural Authority.”

To Updike’s point, Western novels commonly feature central themes to which all symbols and metaphors gravitate. In The Great Gatsby, the notorious green light is unanimously understood as a symbol of Jay Gatbsy’s desperate longing for Daisy, more broadly a symbol of the idealized American Dream. In The Catcher in the Rye, the museum and the carrousel represent the stoppage of time, the preservation of innocence. Similarly, where religion is concerned in Western literature, the shared origin and continuity of the Abrahamic faiths allows for limited interpretations of religious symbols. These interpretations are given fairly specific context by the text and imagery of the Bible, the Torah and the Koran. This is not the case in Japanese literature, especially not in Murakami’s work. I suspect this has much to do with Japanese history and culture. In “Shinto,” the “traditional religion of Japan” (according to Wikipedia), the divine or sacred can manifest in monuments, nature, even people. To borrow from Updike borrowing from Murakami borrowing from Goethe, “Everything’s a metaphor.”

Thus, to approach a Murakami novel with the usual habits of a Western reader – that is, to attempt to identify and grasp every symbol or literary device – is to invite confusion and frustration. Which is why about a quarter of the way through Kafka I dispensed with understanding and allowed myself to be carried along by what one Murakami enthusiast aptly calls the author’s “propulsive force,” that unique trait of his that commands readers to continue on even though they haven’t established perfectly the meaning of a particular scene or character or portion of dialogue. In my opinion, this force is stronger in Kafka than in Wind Up Bird, in part because the reader isn’t pulled away into nearly as many sudden and inexplicable literary back alleys (e.g. the vignettes involving “Boris the Manskinner” and the zoo veterinarian in Wind Up Bird).

The initial intrigue of the story lies in the sudden paralysis of fourteen young boys, one whom, a young Nakata, remains paralyzed much longer than the others, after they encounter an unidentified aircraft during a nature walk somewhere in World War II era Japan. Successive chapters provide additional details of the event, but we’re not given full explanation at any point. It doesn’t really matter, though, because by the time we reach the book’s dramatic conclusion we are sufficiently invested in the mystery of Kafka Tamura’s relationship to Miss Saeki (his mother?) and old man Nakata’s quest to obtain the “entrance stone” (both a lyric in a song written by Miss Saeki years ago and a magical device that will cast open an alternate universe for a purpose that is unknown to all) that we’ve given up speculating about the source of the old man’s powers. This is precisely what is meant by “propulsive force.”

This time around, perhaps in response to certain critical reactions to Wind Up Bird, Murakami seems aware of our overly-technical approach to his writing (thought I can’t be sure he read my Goodreads review). He sprinkles breadcrumbs throughout the plot, many of them references to his own literary influences, to remind us that we shouldn’t be so detached, so analytical. Hiding out in Miss Saeki’s library after running away from his home and school, young Kafka devours Arabian Nights. He describes the book as being “full of obscene, violent, sexual, basically outrageous scenes” with a “vital, living sense of play, of freedom, that common sense can’t keep bottled up.” The boy might as well be describing a Murakami novel; check your common sense at the inside cover.

Later, when discussing Soseki’s The Miner with his librarian friend Oshima, Kafka admits that he doesn’t really know what the author is trying to say but that “not really knowing what he’s getting at is the part that stays with you.” Likewise, when reading Murakami, it is often the expectation of a revelation which may never come (e.g. what actually happened to Nakata on that nature walk when he was a child?) that sustains, even excites, the reader.

Towards the end of the novel, Nakata’s intuition (or magical power) leads him to Miss Saeki’s office, where the nature of their relationship is revealed. Miss Saeki, content that she will soon die, asks Nakata to burn all of her memories, which she has spent decades recording with her Mont Blanc pen. She explains to the unassuming man:

“...writing was important, even though the finished product is completely meaningless.”

Notes on The Catcher In The Rye (Novel, 1951), originally published in September 2018

I’m 33 years old now, so until recently it had been almost 2 decades since I read The Catcher in the Rye. I decided to re-read the book whilst on barracks duty in Okinawa a few months back. For you non-military folk, “duty” is the least desirable garrison-side job in the Marine Corps. You sit at a desk inside a large dormitory building for 24 hours to ensure, primarily, that your compatriots don’t burn the joint down. For me, the occasion meant that I’d have 24 hours - in between frequent perambulations of the hallways and common areas, of course - to knock off a short book.

I chose Catcher because it can be read in a day, and also because I wanted someone, even if only a fictional character like Holden Caulfield, to validate my frustration with the “phonies” who signed me up to work on a Saturday. I expected to be mesmerized all over again by Holden, that legendary hero bum, to align with his sneering distrust of adults and public institutions, to commiserate with him over the bullies and aristocrats at his pasty private school, to resent the high society girls who turn down his polite advancements, to be wooed by his slob philosophy the way I was when I was 13.

But something unexpected occurred when I encountered Holden in my older years. He became, well, sort of dull. What I mean to say is that he’s no longer the paragon of intellectual rebellion that I remembered him to be. His criticism of all things “phony” - his classmates, his Hollywood writer older brother, the sailor home on leave, everyone who’s ever attended an Ivy League institution - is suddenly jejune. Put another way, reading Holden as an adult is kind of like listening to your favorite Sum 41 song from the late 90’s today.

To illustrate my point more clearly, there’s a passage early in the story that affects me now in a way that it didn’t when I was much younger. Holden, having just been bounced from Pencey Prep for putting up miserable grades, decides to trek into New York City. He’ll be able to spend a few days there before his parents learn he’s been expelled and he has to return home. On the train ride into the city, he runs into the mother of one of his classmates. He can’t stand to disappoint the likable woman, so he agrees that her son, Ernest, is a swell guy.But to us he says what he really thinks: “You take a guy like [Ernest] Morrow that’s always snapping their towel at people’s asses - really trying to hurt somebody with it - they don’t just stay a rat while they’re a kid. They stay a rat their whole life.”

I soaked it up as a teenager, wanting to believe in the eternal damnation of my adolescent aggressors. But I don’t think this way anymore, not least because I’m friends with many of the people who either literally or figuratively snapped their towels at me in high school (and vice versa). And clearly this is not an assertion that J.D. Salinger, the authority behind Holden Caulfield, could have possibly believed himself when he wrote the book. Salinger stormed the beaches of Normandy on D-Day. He was present at the Battle of the Bulge and Hurtgen Forest. He saw an entire generation of towel-snappers transformed by the experience of holding their dismembered and dying comrades in their arms. He must've felt that Holden’s condemnation of Ernest rings callow.

Taking Holden’s cynicism at face value requires a degree of naïveté and over-confidence that is characteristic of teens and “tweens” but that, for most of us at least, wears off over time. It isn’t until later in life, when we actually become the target of his pity, indifference or downright condemnation - the debt-ridden college student, the traumatized soldier, the moonlighting cab driver, the overworked school teacher, the obsessed parent - that we realize how shallow our protagonist can be. A part of this misreading of Holden is that we don’t sense, or fully appreciate, his grief; at the age that most of us first encounter Holden, we haven’t yet experienced true loss, let alone the sudden death of a helpless younger sibling. What we see, then, when we first read Holden as high schoolers is not the severely depressed misanthropist he surely is, but, rather, an anti-hero fueled by the supposed wrongs dealt to him by that ubiquitous and nefarious entity: society.

I agree with another critic who describes Holden as a “mirror” to our adolescent selves (though importantly "not a window”). This brings to mind Holden’s remarks about that other great 20th century American novel: “I was crazy about The Great Gatsby. Old Gatsby. Old Sport. That killed me.” Holden deplores the carelessness and profligacy of "The Jazz Age," but he conflates this criticism with Jay Gatsby (and his wealthy vernacular). He doesn’t realize that Gatsby, despite his gaudiness, is as innocent and naïve - as obsessed with preserving something from the past that’s in his eyes pure and good - as he, Holden himself, is. If Gatsby could get Daisy another way, he would. But it’s the interwar years, the roaring twenties, so he buys things - a house with rooms he’s never been in and cars he rarely drives - just to impress her. It’s as genuine, and just as futile, as Holden believing he can remove all the vulgar graffiti off all the walls in all the kindergartens in the world. They’re alike in the ways that should matter to Holden. He just can’t see past the veneer. In this way, Holden reading Gatsby with the limited insight of a teenager is us - that is, our adolescent selves - reading Holden.

So is that the point? Did Salinger write Catcher to show us how gullible we were as teens? I don’t think so. Despite my bloviating about how inexperienced and sheltered and snarky and dishonest and short-sighted and depressed Holden is, I think Salinger contrived him to give us hope.

It’s important to remember that Salinger wrote the book for mature audiences (Portions of the novel were declined by The New Yorker because the Caulfields seemed too precocious [read: adult-like] to be taken seriously). For all his arrogance, Holden is possessed of a pureness and vulnerability which we dismiss as young readers but which later in life we see very clearly and regret not having anymore. It’s funny how I remembered that Holden procures a hooker but not that he refuses to sleep with her, and not that he breaks down in tears over the unscrupulousness of her pimp. These passages, side notes as they were in my youth, stand out to me at age 33 because I’m reminded that in the years since I first met Holden, I’ve become less sensitive to, to some degree even accepting of, the nasty parts of the human condition that he hates so much.

Sure this makes me feel sad and a bit ashamed, but it also makes me want to scrub some of the curse words off the wall. At least the really bad ones.
